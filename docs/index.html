Chef Trends — December 12, 2025

Executive Summary

  • Martini variations and hyper-realistic fruit pastries are dominating upscale menus right now, both offering Instagram-worthy plating moments that justify premium pricing
  • Hot honey has peaked but yuzu is rising fast — operators should transition sweet-heat dishes toward Asian citrus profiles (Monin named yuzu its 2025 Flavor of the Year)
  • Functional beverages with probiotics are moving from wellness niche to mainstream beverage programs, especially for brunch and mocktail menus

Trend 1: Hyper-Realistic Fruit Pastries (Mousse-Based)

  • Platform(s): TikTok, Instagram
  • Velocity: Rising
  • The Hook: Delicate mousse-filled desserts covered in a thin chocolate and cocoa-butter shell, then airbrushed to look like perfect mangoes, strawberries, or peaches. The pastries, only available Saturday and Sunday, have been selling out every weekend at bakeries from Baltimore to LA.
  • Country Club Adaptation: “Market Fruit Trompe-l’Oeil” — Individual lemon or peach mousse pastries (3” diameter) served on slate with edible flower garnish. Present as part of a ladies’ lunch dessert trio or brunch finale. Prep can be done Thursday/Friday for weekend events.
  • Plating Notes: Serve on chilled plates. The “reveal” moment (cutting into realistic fruit to expose mousse) is the Instagram hook. Consider tableside service with small dessert fork and palette knife.
  • Operational Notes: High labor (airbrushing, mold work). Needs pastry chef or trained prep. Make-ahead friendly (freeze shells, assemble day-before). Nut-free versions possible. Gluten-free mousse base. Cost per serving: ~$4-5, sell at $14-16.

Trend 2: Creative Martini Variations (Savory & Dessert Riffs)

  • Platform(s): Restaurant menus, beverage trend reports
  • Velocity: Peaking
  • The Hook: Bars have been putting out inventive takes on the cocktail for the past few years, from Bonnie’s MSG Martini to the Sour Cream & Onion version at The Corner Store. Martini variations appear on trends lists from groups including the National Restaurant Association, Southern Glazer’s Wine & Spirits, and Kimpton Hotels & Restaurants.
  • Country Club Adaptation: “Garden & Grove Martini Flight” — Three 2oz pours: (1) Cucumber-Basil Gin Martini with edible flower, (2) White Peach & Yuzu Vodka Martini, (3) Lavender-Honey Gin Martini. Served on mirrored tray with small amuse (Marcona almonds or cheese crisp). Price at $28 for the flight.
  • Plating Notes: Use coupe or Nick & Nora glasses (more elegant than V-shaped). Garnish with expressed citrus oils, fresh herbs on cocktail picks, or dehydrated citrus wheels. Consistency in glassware temperature (freezer-chilled) is critical.
  • Operational Notes: Batch bases for speed. Pre-infuse spirits (cucumber vodka, lavender gin) weekly. Train bar staff on proper dilution and stirring. Minimal labor day-of. Allergen note: contains alcohol, dairy in some riffs (cream-based).

Trend 3: Yuzu Citrus (Rising Asian Flavor)

  • Platform(s): Restaurant menus, beverage programs, industry forecasts
  • Velocity: Rising
  • The Hook: Monin sets the tone for 2025 with Yuzu as Flavor of the Year — after in-depth consumer research, Monin predicts yuzu to gain even more popularity in 2025. Yuzu, known for being tart with a hint of sweetness and a distinctive floral aroma, is becoming more widely known to U.S. restaurant consumers as Japanese and other Asian cuisines rise in popularity.
  • Country Club Adaptation: “Yuzu-Glazed Scottish Salmon” — Pan-seared salmon with yuzu-miso glaze, served over forbidden rice pilaf and charred broccolini. Finish with microgreens and black sesame. OR “Yuzu Posset Tart” — individual tart shell with yuzu cream, candied lemon peel, and honey tuile.
  • Plating Notes: Yuzu works beautifully in light, bright compositions. Use white or slate plates to showcase color contrast. Yuzu kosho (yuzu-chili paste) can be dotted as accent. Edible flowers (pansies, violas) complement the floral notes.
  • Operational Notes: Yuzu juice/puree is shelf-stable and easy to source (Monin, specialty Asian grocers). Can replace lemon/lime 1:1 in many recipes. Preps well (glazes hold 5 days). Naturally gluten-free, vegan-friendly in most applications. Medium labor.

Trend 4: Burrata Presentations (Elevated, Not Basic)

  • Platform(s): Restaurant menus, Instagram
  • Velocity: Peaking (risk of saturation)
  • The Hook: Burrata is found in 8.6% of U.S. restaurant menus, up more than 30% over the past four years. Burrata cheese, mozzarella stuffed with cream, is an increasing favorite on both casual-dining and upscale-dining restaurant menus, adding a softer, more luxurious and fresh appeal. But critics warn it’s becoming low-effort, high mass-appeal with every restaurant plopping a ball of burrata on a plate with garnish.
  • Country Club Adaptation: “Heirloom Burrata & Stone Fruit Salad” (summer) or “Roasted Beet & Burrata Napoleon” (winter) — layer roasted golden/red beets with burrata, arugula, candied walnuts, and aged balsamic pearls. Build height. Serve with grilled sourdough.
  • Plating Notes: Avoid the “burrata blob” cliché. Cut or tear to expose creamy center. Build vertical (stack components). Use micro-herbs, high-quality olive oil, flaky salt as finish. Contrast textures (crunchy, creamy, fresh).
  • Operational Notes: Purchase burrata fresh (3-day shelf life max). Bring to room temp 30 min before service. Vegetarian. Gluten in bread component (can swap). Medium-high food cost ($3-4 per ball). Sell at $16-19 as appetizer or $24-27 as entrée salad.

Trend 5: Probiotic Functional Beverages (Gut-Health Halo)

  • Platform(s): Beverage menus, wellness culture, retail crossover
  • Velocity: Rising
  • The Hook: The US Probiotic Drink Market is valued at USD 7 billion, primarily driven by increasing consumer awareness of health benefits associated with probiotics, including digestive health and immune support. Probiotic and prebiotic drinks are projected to experience the most rapid growth in the functional beverage market, driven by their broad health benefits.
  • Country Club Adaptation: “Probiotic Brunch Spritz Bar” — Offer 3 housemade options: (1) Ginger-Turmeric Kombucha Spritz with orange, (2) Berry Kefir Smoothie (dairy or coconut), (3) Sparkling Prebiotic “Gut Tonic” (Poppi-style with apple cider vinegar, fruit). Serve in highball glass with fresh fruit garnish. Market as “Wellness Bar” for brunch or post-tennis refreshment.
  • Plating Notes: Clear glassware shows off color. Fresh fruit skewers, edible flowers, herb sprigs elevate presentation. Offer in flights (3x 4oz pours) or full 12oz servings. Label clearly (“Probiotic,” “Supports Digestion”).
  • Operational Notes: Kombucha/kefir can be sourced wholesale or made in-house (requires fermentation knowledge). Shelf-stable syrups (Monin prebiotic line) simplify. Non-alcoholic = no liquor license issues. Vegan, dairy-free options available. Low-moderate labor. $3-4 COGS, sell $8-12.

Trend 6: Hot Honey (Still Relevant, But Transitioning)

  • Platform(s): Restaurant menus, retail products
  • Velocity: Peaking / Saturated
  • The Hook: Hot honey was named one of the Top 10 trends to shape menus next year, according to the National Restaurant Association’s What’s Hot Culinary Forecast. Hot honey has soared in popularity in 2024, with the #hothoney hashtag amassing nearly 100 million views on TikTok, and Google searches never been higher. But it’s now at peak visibility — time to evolve the application.
  • Country Club Adaptation: “Hot Honey & Burrata Flatbread” — wood-fired flatbread with burrata, prosciutto, arugula, hot honey drizzle, and flaky salt. OR “Hot Honey Glazed Brussels Sprouts” with crispy pancetta and parmesan. The key: pair with premium ingredients to elevate beyond “trendy condiment.”
  • Plating Notes: Use squeeze bottles for controlled drizzle (visual appeal). Contrast sweet/spicy with creamy (burrata, goat cheese) or rich (bacon, nuts). Garnish with fresh herbs (basil, thyme) to add sophistication.
  • Operational Notes: Hot honey is shelf-stable, easy to source (Mike’s Hot Honey, JD’s). Can be housemade (honey + chili flakes simmered). Versatile across apps, entrees, desserts. No major allergens (check for gluten cross-contact in facility). Low labor. Medium food cost.

Skip These

  • Cottage Cheese Everything — According to TikTok’s 2023 Year on TikTok report, “cottage cheese in everything” was the #1 trend of 2023, from bread to ice cream to cookie dough. Now oversaturated; reads as last season for upscale dining. Skip unless reimagined as hidden ingredient (e.g., in mousse base).

  • Tinned Fish Boards — While regenerative seafood is rising, the “tinned fish aesthetic board” trend has peaked in NYC/coastal markets. Country club audiences may find it too casual or polarizing. Instead, incorporate quality tinned items into composed dishes (conserva tuna crudo, anchovy vinaigrette).

  • Sleepy Girl Mocktails — The Sleepy Girl Mocktail saw massive resurgence during Dry January 2024, with the hashtag showing up on almost 10,000 posts, but it’s tied to a specific wellness micro-trend (tart cherry juice + magnesium). Too niche and “TikTok-coded” for refined club dining. Stick to broader functional beverage category.
